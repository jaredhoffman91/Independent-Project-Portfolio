NHL Draft Analytics · 2010–2020 · 2,300+ Players

Which NHL organizations actually develop the players they draft?

Reputation gets treated as evidence in hockey conversation constantly. The Organizational Development Index measures it instead: how much NHL value an organization's drafted players produce relative to what their draft position predicted, and how much of that value the organization actually kept versus lost through trades.

Credit shouldn't outlive control

Most draft analysis credits a team forever for a player it drafted, even decades after a trade sends him elsewhere. That measures scouting, not development.

Development Credit counts a player's full NHL value toward the team that drafted him, regardless of trades.

In-Organization Credit only counts value produced while the player was actually on that team's roster.

The gap between the two tells you how much of a team's apparent success is actually happening somewhere else.

What the data actually found

Traded-Player Effect

Nashville's ranking drops nearly 90% once trades are accounted for

Nashville's Development Credit efficiency (1.9) collapses to 0.2 under In-Organization Credit, the largest shift in the dataset, driven largely by value produced after key trades.

Never Played A Game

Filip Forsberg never played a single game for Washington

Filip Forsberg was traded before ever playing a game for Washington. Their credit for him reflects a scouting decision, not development, since the organization never actually had the chance to develop him.

Signal vs. Noise

Detroit's low rank is real. Vegas's isn't, yet.

Detroit ranks near the bottom on the largest sample in the study (94 picks) — a real signal. Vegas ranks low too, but with only 20 picks from 3–4 draft classes as a 2017 expansion team, too small a sample to separate weakness from noise.

Diagnostic, Not Just A Ranking

Chicago develops forwards well and defensemen poorly, in the same organization

Forward efficiency of 3.46 sits next to a defense efficiency of -0.54, the worst defensive mark in the study. A single overall number would hide this. The positional breakdown doesn't.

What this doesn't measure

This model only tracks NHL-level games, so time spent developing in the AHL is invisible regardless of which organization is responsible for it. It can't fully separate scouting skill from development skill for a player who's never traded. Point Shares, the underlying value metric, underweights defensive contribution relative to offense, a known limitation of most public hockey statistics. Full limitations, methodology, and a complete data-cleaning log are in the paper.
